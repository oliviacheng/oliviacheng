Background/Research

        Safety is a prominent issue, especially in Cal. We joke about getting chased down the street by strangers and getting mugged late at night, but the dark humor is based on true stories. For this hackathon, our team decided to address the issue of safety with an app. Accidon't strays from your average navigation app and helps a user avoid crime centric areas.

        For research, I drafted a quick survey for Cal students to take on safety on campus versus safety commuting home. <https://docs.google.com/forms/d/1tzuwgK0o9n_HJQ1yMx4vStGxesP0mZSZVvd8wDnRgmc/edit> Students felt safe on campus, but unsafe on the commute home. Most used google maps or apple maps when tackling navigation.

Analyzing Numbers:

There is a disparity between how safe people feel on campus versus off. This shift brings to attention a problem that we need to solve.

Most students record staying on campus until 6pm or later. Accidents are prone to happen later at night after sunset.

Common commute back home include walking and/or public transport. More accidents happen when commuters are alone on foot.

How does it work?

        The app takes crime data from public datasets and ranks safety of all possible routes from Point A to Point B based on how many crimes were reported on each route. Each route is given a crime score from 1-5, one being the safest possible route. Given more time, we would’ve liked to implement a ranking system for the type of crime committed, ranging from the most to least physically harmful. The app would also warn the user of crimes committed live if they are near the route they are on so users can steer clear of the area and look more into the crime if they wanted to. Finally, we wanted to implement the Augmented Reality function Mapbox enabled. Research shows that users who use AR are more interactive with and focused on their surroundings than users who rely on 2D mapping (Ryokai 2013). Being on your phone can also be dangerous when one is in an unfamiliar place because the user is less aware of the environment. AR can be a solution to this caveat.

Low-fi Wireframing

        I first took the time to consider what the user flow would actually look like on a high level. I drafted features that were necessary like the maps portion, easy access to an overview of directions, crime alerts, and an easy way to view criminal activity.

High-fi Wireframing

        I used Figma to create a high-fidelity prototype that mapped out the user flow. I also used this prototype to present the app to the judges. I focused on teasing out the basics of the app and what features would be used the most. The most challenging part was prototyping the app to work like how people would expect it to when using it.

Play around with the app here: https://www.figma.com/file/7nITkhclNXCHLd6EQo0Tln/SafeMapps?node-id=0%3A1

User Testing

I changed/added a couple features after testing the app out with a couple hackers at the event.

  • The directions box had a ‘minimize’ function at the top right corner, but users kept pressing the ‘3 dots’ button that opened it in the first place in order to close it. I scraped the minimize function and prototyped the ‘3 dots’ button to be able to open and close the menu.
  • Users searched to play around with different modes of transport, so I added a button in the directions menu that could allow a user to backtrack and change the mode of transportation.
  • The ‘GO’ button is not prominent enough and the ambiguity slows the user flow. (Maybe change to a different color or shape so that it doesn’t blend in with the background).

        I would’ve liked to develop the AR features a bit more and built out the crime alert function.

Future Applications

        Though the app caters to University/College students, we were thinking it could be advantageous to tourists as well as anyone who uses navigation apps (which is over three-fourths of smartphone users (Panko 2018)). An add-on that maps routes according to safety levels would be useful to people who are new to an area, traveling, exploring, or just going home on a Friday night.
